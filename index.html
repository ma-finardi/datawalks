Tree

Tap ▶ to play · drag the bar to seek · tap Read transcript to expand the text

Introduction

Read transcript

Your world is a set of points tracing the surface of a tree trunk. In this world a point as a sphere with no volume, collapsed in its center, that never gets bigger if you approach it. Each point has 3 fundamental characteristics: x, y and z.

Three letters represent the relationship between the point and the three dimensional space it is placed within. X tells left-right position. Y tells forward-back. And z tells up-down.

This data world is a small world. For us humans, its boundaries are outside our perception. The distance between points can be one tenth of a millimetre or less. Something we can make sense of, but hardly perceive.

Many features of this world are too small for human perception. The cloud of points lets humans explore a tree trunk in ways not accessible to unaided senses.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

Look around you and find a tree.

How can you look at the points? What features do they refer to?

Dataworld exploration

Read transcript

The story of this world is traced by a laser. A sharp, narrow, linear beam of light spinning and rotating. By projecting the laser onto its surroundings, the lidar scanner times the reflection of its beam from the tree surfaces to make a cloud of points.

Looking at the tree — from multiple points of view, the Lidar and the humans operating it are able to create a ghost of the tree trunk. Millions of points refer to millions of locations. Organized in digital space according to their coordinates.

This cloud samples the exterior, not the insides and not the deep folds. Until two algorithms enter the scene. An algorithm can fit a surface to these points. One connects the points to each other, creating lines and surfaces — the surface modelling algorithm. Triangles where each point is a vertex are created, or curved lines that attempt to capture the flow of the original tree bark.

This world is now populated by sparse points and by new surfaces. This surface turns, tilts, leans and curves, twists and breaks between smooth and rough areas.

Now the world is made of points, of surfaces, and of curvatures. The algorithm now looks at changes in curvature, and identifies distinct features of the tree that were reflected by the laser. Finally the cloud of points is categorized. Each point now belongs to a surface and to its features, and can be traced back to their specific story.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

You are with a tree.

Can you see the features on its surface? How many features can you identify?

Meanings and stories

Read transcript

This data world allows humans to explore the life of the tree. Each feature is an element of a tree shape produced through a story of endurance, adaptation, and change. Each feature opens onto physical memories of relationships with other entities.

Observable patterns produced by past or ongoing processes. Hollows in the wood sheltered beetles. Beetles, stick insects, moths fed on the tree's leaves and deposited larvae in the tree, making a nesting habitat.

These tunnels are many, as the stories are plural: each beetle had its own relationships, conflicts and collaborations with the tree. The traces of these insects can also reflect broader entanglements with the surrounding ecosystem, acting as a potential signal for anthropogenic disturbances.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

You notice the features on the tree.

Do features suggest stories? Can you try to tell some?

Outro and provocations

Read transcript

This dataworld enables knowledge of a set of points, surfaces and patterns. From there, the interpretation of a possible story of the tree and its interaction with the ecologies it is interconnected with.

What does this data-world leave out? It is the world of the surface. It has finite resolution. It is a static state at the moment of capture. Humans will not know what is going on inside the tree. This Lidar agent will not see what is smaller than 0.1 millimeters. And this point cloud exists in a stable state that freezes the here and now of when it was captured.

We will not know the stories of the infinitely small — of microbes and microorganisms inhabiting the same surface, flattened into two dimensions. The bird's song, the wind in the leaves, the smell of the bark, the flow of sap are all missing.

But maybe, we don't need to know everything. And each dataset is as good as it is imperfect. Each database is imperfect, but some are useful. Ultimately we are asking: in what is this dataset wrong? And in what is it useful?

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

You take a final look at the tree.

What features are missing? What escapes your understanding?

Use your postcard to sketch your observations.

Galaxies

Tap ▶ to play · drag the bar to seek · tap Read transcript to expand the text

Introduction

Read transcript

Your world is made up of pixels. Pixels forming an image of a patch in the sky. This image is a data visualisation of what astronomers use to study distant galaxies, galaxies as a mix of stars, gas, dust and dark matter.

In this patch of sky are galaxies that are close to us. And then there are some that are far away beyond the Milky Way.

These pixels came from the past, a collection of galaxies from 11 billion years ago, when the universe itself would have been only a couple of billions years old. Stars that have since evolved because light takes time to reach us.

The time and space in scale to our own physical environment, and bodies, cannot be easily translated. Instead, we rely on telescopes and visualisations to gain a better understanding of these galaxies and our universe.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

You may not be able to see the stars where you are.

But, what relationship do you have with the sky? Do you remember the stories of constellations and planets?

Dataworld exploration

Read transcript

The story of this world started with a patch of the sky. A field of view. There was a telescope from a space observatory that gathered the data.

Light in the sky converted to numbers and then reconverted to image, light again in another form. Converting raw data into something that is scientifically usable and meaningful.

There are pipelines, processes, used to clean this dataset. Cleaning removes artefacts related to the instrument, the background of the sky, in order to get a clean picture of the patch, a clean picture of the field of view.

With the image of the field of view we can begin making sense of the galaxies in our sky.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

Look around. You are always surrounded by 'raw data', even from where you are listening.

When you see raw data what do you do?

Meanings and stories

Read transcript

In this image there are galaxies close to us, and those that are far far away. The image is made of pixel formations. You can see lights in different sizes and brightness. You can count the points of a star.

And in the right corner of this image you can see a big arc. A cluster of light sprayed across the space. This large arc is formed because of the ways the galaxies have been captured in the moment.

The arc represents the effect when a big galaxy, or a cluster of galaxies are in front and behind each other in the image. And because of the gravitational effects the light rays get distorted forming this shape, a stroke of light sources.

This structure is three dimensional, allowing you to take a closer look at the internal structure of this faraway galaxy. This galaxy was found when the universe was primordial, when the universe was young.

When you look closely at the arc, there are light sources that form little peaks, star forming regions. An image of a field of view that tells us about the formation and decay of stars and galaxies, close to the whole formation of our own universe.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

The data around you can be felt.

What senses do you use to notice patterns and connections? Do you rely on visualisations, sonifications, or something else completely different?

Outro and provocations

Read transcript

Not all datasets represent environments, objects or more-than-human entities that we can experience solely with our own bodies and senses.

Yet many datasets do reach us as numbers, pixels or sound waves that are still raw and need further cleaning to help us make sense of the information.

In the image of a field of view, of a patch in the sky, we see a dataset that coexists of different timelines. A dataworld beyond human scales and temporalities, inviting us to navigate different ways of knowing.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

You take one final look at your surroundings.

How do you relate to the data around you? Use the postcard to write your reflections.

Wetland

Tap ▶ to play · drag the bar to seek · tap Read transcript to expand the text

Introduction

Read transcript

Your world is made up ecological relationships in a wetland ecosystem.

People collaborated to create this data world in order to understand vital relationships within this place.

A place that is entangled in human and non-human connections because the wetland is also connected to a corporation.

In this world the ecological data is trying to advocate for endangered and connected species that may be overlooked and get lost in the sets of spreadsheets and online platforms available to the public.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

You look around the landscape you're in.

What human and non-humans can you see? How could they be connected to each other?

Dataworld exploration

Read transcript

The story of this world starts with platforms and databases where you can download information about species living in a specific location. Species are the names given to the more-than-human inhabitants of this vast network of relationships. Inhabitants that interact and relate to one another.

The world begins with rows and columns, neatly lined up, one by one. A spreadsheet of data entries counting species, locations and other ecological categories. The lines spread along describing a wetland and its species. A frog, a bat, a turtle.

Inside each entry is one kind of relationship. Each column a characteristic of a connection from one species to another. For example the wood turtle eats red raspberries. The little brown bat roosts with an Indiana bat. And the spotted turtle is eaten by a musk rat.

You walk along a column of birds, plants and insects. You see a raccoon. You notice the relationships look like a web of networks, creating a kind of synthetic index of biodiversity.

Lines are converging, forging new pathways to follow. The lines look less structured than the rows and columns behind you. You follow a bright blue line. It leads you to a bog turtle.

These nodes and connections are leading you towards a larger network. A network put together by people, people using physical threads to make sense of the wetland.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

What kind of relationships between species can you see?

Between a tree and a bird, or the grass and the soil. Would you use threads or other tools to highlight relationships?

Meanings and stories

Read transcript

From the bog turtle's view there is a horizon full of other colour threads. On your left you can see green lines leading towards vegetation. On your right, a red line leading towards a predator. You can also see more blue lines heading to other species.

You walk towards the node that brings you to a raccoon, commonly known as a trash panda. The thread ahead looks like a thick cluster of connections, forming a winding sphere. Over there the lines overlap and encroach each other, forming bounded threads. The nodes form a circle, connecting and reconnecting to reinforce new patterns.

New patterns and connections of relationships between plants, birds, turtles and more. Pulling at these threads provide alternative ways of seeing the wetland. Organising and disorganising, disorientating and orientating.

These clusters show how interconnected these species are, how important their relationships are in making the wetland sustainable. Different kinds of biodiversity data that brings the most endangered species to the foreground. Seeing the wetland through their view of entangled relational connections.

Biodiversity data can often be indexed and flattened, creating generalisations and averages. Quantifying species, and leaving out data that stands out, or sits outside the bell curve of expectations. But this data set forces you to see beyond static numbers, but how they also relate. Allowing you to see a wetland's network without visiting. A dataset anyone can access and make indirect observations of a place.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

The species in front of you don't exist alone. They are entangled in a network of their own. You are now entangled as well.

How do you connect your body to the environment around you?

Outro and provocations

Read transcript

Outside the threads are gaps. Nodes that don't connect to other species. Threads of connections that don't align with others because their ecological interactions were not counted for.

You take a step outside the pathways weaving into each other. Into the space where there are no nodes, no connections. Are all relationships between bats and owls the same?

You keep walking in between the threads, encountering others. Datasets are constructed and abstracted and reconstructed again to help us find new meanings. But refinements of data can't always represent everything that exists in a landscape.

Take a few minutes to engage with this prompt.

You take one final look at your surroundings.

What other connections would you make? Use the postcard to record your reflections.
